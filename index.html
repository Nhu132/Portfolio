The situation

Gremsy makes gimbal stabilizers and camera payloads for industrial drone applications — inspection, mapping, surveillance, agriculture. When I joined, the brand had a well-maintained YouTube presence but the content wasn't speaking to the people who actually buy: systems integrators, enterprise drone operators, and industrial procurement teams. Content was product-centric — specs and features — with very little that answered "why does this matter to my operation?"

How I thought about it

I started by researching not the product — but the buyers. I read how industrial players talked about their own work: what problems they named, what they cared about in field operations, why data security mattered in aerial surveillance contexts.

I noticed a pattern: the company was chasing competitors' product launches instead of building its own authority. The shift I pushed for: move from "here's what our product does" to "here's the problem our customers solve, and here's how we fit into that."

"B2B buyers don't want to be impressed — they want to know if you understand their problem."
What I did
Social repositioning

Rebuilt LinkedIn editorial calendar around solution-first content. Contacted partners (Sony Europe, Nokia for Industries, HHLA Sky) to source real deployment imagery and co-create posts that showed Gremsy in enterprise contexts — building credibility by proximity.

Content rewrite

Rewrote product copy to answer application questions: which payload for which integrator type, what environments each product is built for, what data workflows it supports. Spec-listing became context-setting.

Lead funnel ownership

As HubSpot super admin, built the end-to-end lead capture and nurture flow — segmentation, newsletter sends, open/click tracking — and defined the handoff point to Sales via Zoho CRM. This required understanding where in the buyer journey a lead was "sales-ready."

What I'd do differently

Sales and Marketing met regularly, and the team brought valuable frontline context. But looking back, I didn't always have a clear way to separate what Sales perceived about buyers from what buyers actually said about themselves. If I were to do this again, I'd supplement internal input with direct buyer signals — through content engagement patterns, newsletter reply tracking, or short feedback loops at events — to build a more independent view of what the audience actually needed at each stage.

How I think

This is how I approach B2B content — not from a textbook, but from working inside a hardware tech company selling to enterprise buyers across 40+ countries.

01

B2B buyers don't buy products. They buy the reduction of risk.

In B2C, the worst outcome of a bad purchase is personal disappointment. In B2B, a wrong decision affects operations, budget cycles, and the buyer's credibility inside their own organization. That's not rational calculation — that's fear.

At Gremsy, I stopped writing about gimbal specs and started writing about what happens when a drone inspection fails mid-flight over a wind turbine. That reframe changed everything — because now the content was speaking to the fear, not the feature.

Jobs-to-be-done Buyer psychology Solution framing
02

B2B content earns trust through specificity, not volume.

B2C content can afford to be broad — reach enough people and conversion follows. B2B doesn't work that way. Your audience is small, highly informed, and immediately skeptical of generic claims.

What builds trust: using the right technical terminology, showing the product working in conditions they recognize, and letting your partners speak for you. Who you work with tells buyers more than what you say about yourself.

03

How I start when there's nothing yet.

If I were handed a B2B SaaS with no existing content strategy, this is where I'd begin — and why.

1 —

Read the ecosystem before touching the product

Partners' websites, competitors' campaigns, the language customers use in reviews. I'm looking for patterns: what problems get named repeatedly, what the category leader is saying — and what gap they're leaving open.

2 —

Mine Sales and Support before writing a single brief

Not to get their opinions on content — to hear the exact words customers use when they're confused, hesitant, or won over. That language is the brief.

3 —

Ask why, not what

When I look at a competitor's campaign, I don't just note what they're doing. I ask: why this message now? What customer anxiety are they addressing? That's where the strategic gaps — and opportunities — actually live.

The honest gap

I haven't yet had the environment to build a full-funnel content system with clean attribution and pipeline metrics. That's what I'm looking for next — a company where content is taken seriously enough to be measured properly.

Writing samples

B2B content written for Gremsy JSC — an industrial drone hardware company selling to systems integrators, enterprise operators, and procurement teams globally. All pieces written in English, without a dedicated editor.

Partner announcement Oct 2024

Gremsy × HHLA Sky: Industrial inspection partnership

Audience: Enterprise drone operators, industrial procurement teams
Goal: Build enterprise credibility via association — show Gremsy operating at port-scale infrastructure with a certified German MRM operator
Challenge: Translate technical integration (IEC 62443-certified ICC + Pixy LR S-Port + Sony ILX-LR1) into readable narrative without losing credibility
Highlight passage
"The ICC enables the remote (BVLOS) control of more than 100 drone operations simultaneously from a centralized control station, eliminating bottlenecks associated with traditional manual inspections…"

Leads with operational scale (100 drones, BVLOS), then the security credential enterprise buyers need, then deployment scope. This is the buyer's decision checklist, in order.

Product launch Oct 2024

Two-axis Pixy LR — product announcement

Audience: Systems integrators, infrastructure inspection professionals
Goal: Lead with the operational problem (obstructed field of view), not the spec sheet
Approach: Structured around 6 specific use cases so different integrators could self-identify their scenario immediately
Highlight passage
"Conventional gimbal systems, often limited by airframe components or payload placement, struggle to achieve this level of coverage… Two-axis Pixy LR eliminates these limitations, streamlining workflows and reducing operational time and complexity."

Names the competitor pain point explicitly before introducing the solution. Integrators immediately recognize their current frustration — that recognition earns attention in B2B content.

Solution overview Jul 2024

Gremsy solutions for Sony ILX-LR1

Audience: Drone integrators evaluating gimbal compatibility
Goal: Guide integrators toward the right Gremsy variant based on their platform (MAVLink, DJI M300/M350, open platform)
Note: One of my earlier pieces — the intro leans on marketing language. The stronger section is the product comparison matrix in the body.
What I'd write differently now

I'd open with the integrator's decision problem — "You've chosen the Sony ILX-LR1. Now you need a gimbal that fits your drone platform without rebuilding your integration stack" — rather than leading with Gremsy's own claims. The body structure is sound; the intro needed a sharper entry point.
