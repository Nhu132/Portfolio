The situation

Gremsy's content was product-centric — specs and features — not speaking to the real buyers: systems integrators, enterprise drone operators, industrial procurement teams.

The pattern I noticed

The company was chasing competitors' launches instead of building authority. Content answered "what does it do?" instead of "why does this matter to my operation?"

"B2B buyers don't want to be impressed — they want to know if you understand their problem."

🔗

Social repositioning

Rebuilt LinkedIn editorial calendar around solution-first content. Sourced real deployment imagery from partners (Sony Europe, Nokia for Industries, HHLA Sky) to build credibility by proximity.

✍️

Content rewrite

Rewrote product copy to answer application questions: which payload for which integrator type, what environments each product is built for, what data workflows it supports.

📊

Lead funnel ownership

As HubSpot super admin — lead capture, segmentation, newsletter sends, open/click tracking, and defined the sales-ready handoff point to Zoho CRM.

What I'd do differently

Sales and Marketing met regularly and brought valuable context. But I didn't always have a clear way to separate what Sales perceived about buyers from what buyers actually said. Next time: supplement internal input with direct buyer signals — engagement patterns, newsletter replies, event feedback — to build a more independent view.

02 — Case study

Owning a product launch
end to end

2-Axis Pixy LR · Gremsy JSC · Oct 2024 · Role: sole content & launch owner

The challenge

Launch a technically complex new gimbal variant — 7 deliverables across 5 channels — in under 2 weeks, coordinating R&D, design, CS, and Sales with no dedicated project manager.

What made it hard

The product had nuanced technical differentiators (SWaP architecture, 2-axis vs 3-axis tradeoffs, rover-mounted use cases) that required deep understanding before a single word of copy could be written.

🔬

Research & messaging foundation

Sat with R&D to understand the product technically, then researched industrial players to map the real use cases buyers care about — bridge inspection, wind turbine blades, drilling rigs, confined spaces. This research shaped every piece of copy that followed.

📋

Launch proposal & timeline

Built the full launch plan: 7 deliverables mapped to a single timeline with clear ownership and deadlines. Identified dependencies — Quick Start content from CS, pricing from product team — and flagged them early to avoid last-minute blockers.

✍️

Content across all channels

Wrote the news article, newsletter copy, and social posts — each with a different angle. The article led with operational use cases. The newsletter focused on the key differentiator (unobstructed view vs conventional gimbals). Social was structured for quick scanning with the most visual proof points first.

🎬

Media briefing

Briefed design and video team with enough technical and strategic context that they could produce on-brand visuals without back-and-forth — product angles that showed the front-mount design clearly, video scripted around the inspection use case narrative.

What this showed me

Product launches in B2B hardware are only partly about content quality — they're mostly about sequencing, dependency management, and knowing which questions to ask R&D before it's too late to change the messaging. The content is the last 20%. The other 80% is understanding the product well enough to make the right calls when nobody's telling you what to do.

03 — How I think

B2B content is about
your buyer's fear of being wrong.

Not from a textbook — from working inside a hardware tech company selling to enterprise buyers across 40+ countries.

01

B2B buyers don't buy products. They buy the reduction of risk.

In B2B, a wrong decision affects operations, budget cycles, and the buyer's credibility inside their own organization. That's not rational calculation — that's fear.

At Gremsy, I stopped writing about gimbal specs and started writing about what happens when a drone inspection fails over a wind turbine. Content was now speaking to the fear, not the feature.

02

B2B content earns trust through specificity, not volume.

Your audience is small, highly informed, and skeptical of generic claims. What builds trust: technical terminology that signals you understand their world, real-world application proof, and who you partner with — that tells buyers more than anything you say about yourself.

03 — How I start from zero
1

Read the ecosystem before touching the product

Partners' sites, competitors' campaigns, customer language in reviews. Looking for patterns: what problems get named repeatedly, what the category leader is saying — and what gap they're leaving open.

2

Mine Sales and Support before writing a single brief

Not for their content opinions — for the exact words customers use when confused, hesitant, or won over. That language is the brief.

3

Ask why, not what

When I look at a competitor's campaign: why this message now? What customer anxiety are they addressing? That's where strategic gaps actually live.

The honest gap

I haven't yet had the environment to build a full-funnel system with clean attribution and pipeline metrics. That's what I'm looking for — a company where content is taken seriously enough to be measured properly.

Technical enablement 2025

VIO — Common Issues and Solutions

Audience: Systems integrators and drone operators onboarding Gremsy's VIO gimbal payload
Source material: Raw troubleshooting notes from the Customer Support team — written for internal use, not for customers
What I did: Restructured 9 distinct technical topics (web server setup, AutoPilot connection, IP/UDP config, control modes, camera settings) into a scannable slide-based guide. Rewrote CS language into operator-friendly copy while keeping technical accuracy — IP addresses, port numbers, protocol names — intact. Also handled the visual design and layout of the entire deck.
Why this matters
This piece sits at the intersection of content strategy and technical writing — taking something only CS understood and making it usable by the people who actually need it in the field. The challenge wasn't writing. It was knowing which details to keep, which to simplify, and which order a confused operator would need them in.
